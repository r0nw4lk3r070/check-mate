# Oil inventories draining at record pace — Europe's energy security on the line

**The IEA's May 2026 Oil Market Report confirms the worst: global oil stocks are depleting at a record rate, the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively shut, and Europe faces months of severe undersupply — with no strategic autonomy to fall back on.**

## What is being said

The International Energy Agency released its May 2026 Oil Market Report on 13 May, confirming that global oil inventories fell by 129 million barrels in March and a further 117 million barrels in April — a cumulative drawdown of 250 million barrels in just two months. The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since 2 March, cutting off roughly 12.8 million barrels per day of supply. Brent crude trades above $107 per barrel.

IEA oil markets division head Toril Bosoni stated: "Even if there is a solution to the conflict, we do think it will take time — weeks and months — to resume flows through the Strait of Hormuz to a normal pace." The agency projects the market will remain "severely undersupplied" until October even if the conflict ends soon.

## What the evidence shows

The data is stark:

- **Supply losses**: Global oil supply declined by a further 1.8 million barrels per day in April, bringing total losses since the Iran war began on 28 February to 12.8 million bpd. Gulf countries affected by the Hormuz closure are producing 14.4 million bpd below pre-war levels.
- **Inventory drawdown**: Global observed stocks fell at roughly 4 million bpd in March and April. OECD on-land stocks collapsed by 146 million barrels in April alone (4.9 million bpd). The IEA warns that OECD commercial inventories are on track to approach operational stress levels by early June.
- **Strategic reserves activated**: 32 IEA member countries have released a combined 400 million barrels from commercial and government strategic storage. The US Strategic Petroleum Reserve is functioning as designed, but cannot bridge a 10+ million bpd gap indefinitely.
- **Demand destruction**: World oil consumption is set to plunge by 2.45 million bpd this quarter — the steepest drop since the 2020 COVID pandemic. The IEA now forecasts global demand contracting by 420,000 bpd year-on-year in 2026, down 1.3 million bpd from pre-war projections.
- **Price pressure**: JPMorgan's first formal oil price forecast in two months projects Brent averaging $96/bbl in 2026 and $75/bbl in 2027 — but the current spot price above $107 reflects the acute supply gap.
- **Cumulative losses**: Total supply losses already exceed 1 billion barrels. Morgan Stanley forecasts an additional billion barrels lost over the course of 2026 due to the time required to restart oilfields, repair refineries, and reposition the tanker fleet.

## European context

This crisis exposes Europe's energy vulnerability in the starkest possible terms. The continent spent years reducing its dependence on Russian gas after 2022 — only to discover that oil supply chains are just as fragile when a single chokepoint closes.

The IEA's coordinated reserve release is an emergency measure, not a strategy. Europe has no sovereign oil backstop comparable to the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve. European strategic petroleum reserves are fragmented across 27 member states, with no centralised deployment mechanism.

Meanwhile, the broader pattern is unmistakable: when the Strait of Hormuz shut, European leaders were not at the table where the response was designed. The IEA response was led by the United States. Spain's Foreign Minister Albares explicitly called for an EU army this week, citing doubts over US defence commitments. Europe watches the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing from the sidelines, as rare earths and critical minerals — the building blocks of energy transition — are negotiated without European input.

Strategic autonomy is not an abstract debate. It is the difference between being at the table and being on the menu. The oil crisis proves that energy security is defence security — and Europe still does not have either one under sovereign control.
