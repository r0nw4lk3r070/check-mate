# From Procurement to Power: The New Logic of European Defence

**The launch of the European Defence Industry Programme (EDIP) marks a fundamental shift in how Europe views its security: moving from fragmented national shopping lists to a unified industrial strategy.**

## What is being said

For decades, European defence was characterized by "interoperability" — the hope that different national tanks and jets could somehow work together in a crisis. The narrative was one of coordination, not integration. The primary goal was to ensure that Member States didn't buy too many different types of the same thing.

## What the evidence shows

The adoption of the EUR 1.5 billion multiannual work programme under the **European Defence Industry Programme (EDIP)** in March 2026 signals the end of the "shopping list" era. 

The evidence shows a pivot toward three systemic capabilities:
- **Ramping Production Capacity**: EDIP is not just buying equipment; it is funding the *capacity* to produce. By dedicating over EUR 700 million to reinforcing production, the EU is treating the defence industry as a strategic utility rather than a series of vendors.
- **Joint Procurement as a Standard**: The move toward joint procurement between Member States reduces redundant spending and forces the industry to standardize at the design level, not just the operational level.
- **Technological Edge**: Specifically targeting anti-drone systems and missile production, the EU is focusing on the "attrition gaps" revealed by the conflict in Ukraine.

## European context

This is the "industrialization of sovereignty." While the political debate about a "European Army" often gets bogged down in sovereignty fears, the *industrial* reality of a European Defence Technological and Industrial Base (EDTIB) is quietly creating the infrastructure of a federal security architecture.

You cannot have a unified strategic posture if you have 27 different supply chains for the same missile. By integrating the industrial base via EDIP, the EU is effectively building the "nervous system" of a future European Defence Union.

Strategic autonomy is no longer just a slogan from Paris; it is now a line item in the EU budget. This is the bridge between the current Union of states and a future Federation of security.
