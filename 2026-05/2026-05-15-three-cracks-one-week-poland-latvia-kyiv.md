# Three Cracks in One Week — US Troops Cancelled, a Government Falls, Kyiv Burns

**In the same week that Russia launched the largest aerial assault of the entire war, the United States cancelled a major troop deployment to Poland and Latvia's government collapsed over drone spillover from the conflict. Europe's security architecture is being tested from three directions at once.**

## What is happening

Three events converged in the week of May 12–15, 2026, each exposing a different fracture in Europe's security:

1. **US cancels 4,000-troop deployment to Poland.** On May 14, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth abruptly cancelled a long-planned nine-month rotation of 4,000 Texas-based troops to Poland — even though troops and equipment had already started arriving. The decision caught Pentagon officials and European allies completely by surprise. "We had no idea this was coming," one US defence official told Politico. The move follows Hegseth's earlier announcement to withdraw 5,000 troops from Germany, itself a response to Chancellor Merz calling the US Iran operation "humiliating." Trump has labelled Poland a "model ally" for its high defence spending — and still pulled the deployment. Lt. Gen. Ben Hodges, former commander of US Army Europe, said: "The Army's role in Europe is all about deterring the Russians, protecting America's strategic interests and assuring allies. And now a very important asset that was coming to be part of that deterrence is gone."

2. **Latvia's government falls over drone spillover.** Prime Minister Evika Siliņa resigned on May 14 after firing Defence Minister Andris Sprūds over the handling of Ukrainian drones that strayed into Latvian territory from Russia. On May 7, two drones exploded at an oil storage facility in Latvia. The army had failed to detect them. Siliņa's coalition partner, the Progressives, withdrew support over the sacking, leaving her without a majority. Ukraine's foreign minister Andrii Sybiha said the intrusions were "the result of Russian electronic warfare deliberately diverting Ukrainian drones from their targets in Russia." This is the first European government to fall as a direct consequence of Russia's war spilling into NATO territory.

3. **Russia's largest aerial assault of the war.** On May 13–14, Russia launched over 1,560 drones and missiles at Ukraine in the largest single aerial assault since the full-scale invasion began. A ballistic missile strike on a residential building in Kyiv killed at least 24 people. Poland scrambled fighter jets; Slovakia closed its border with Ukraine; Moldova reported a Russian drone crossing 180 miles through its airspace. Hungary's PM Péter Magyar summoned the Russian ambassador — a historic first for Budapest.

## What the evidence shows

The three events are not coordinated, but they compound. Russia is testing how far it can push — in the air, across borders, and in the information space — while the United States is signalling that European security is conditional on political compliance.

The Poland deployment cancellation is particularly striking because it targets America's most loyal European ally. Poland spends over 4% of GDP on defence, hosts US troops willingly, and has never publicly criticised Trump. As Hodges noted: "The Poles certainly have never criticized President Trump, and they do all the things that good allies are supposed to do. And yet, this happens." The message to every European capital is unambiguous: no amount of compliance guarantees American commitment.

The Latvia collapse reveals a different vulnerability. A NATO member state's government fell because it could not detect or intercept drones crossing its territory — drones that were not even targeting Latvia. Russia's electronic warfare is pushing Ukrainian drones off course and into NATO airspace, and the alliance has no systematic answer.

The Kyiv strike — 24 civilians killed in an apartment building — is the human cost that underpins both the military and political fractures. Each escalation tests whether the West will respond. So far, Russia's calculation appears to be that it will not.

## European context

These three cracks converge on one question: can Europe defend itself without relying on American decisions made in Washington?

The B9+Nordic summit in Bucharest on May 12 committed to 5% GDP defence spending. The EU issued a €10B defence bond, 16x oversubscribed. Zelenskyy announced a 13-country anti-ballistic coalition. Finland's NATOpoll showed 79% distrust in US defence commitments. Spain's foreign minister called for a European army.

All of these were signals of intent. The events of May 14–15 are the test of whether that intent is real. The US is pulling troops from the continent. A European government has collapsed. Civilians are dying in numbers not seen since the war's first months.

Europe has the money. It has the institutional framework. It does not yet have the political will to act at the speed this moment demands.
