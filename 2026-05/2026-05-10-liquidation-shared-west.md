# The Liquidation of the Shared West and the Case for European Autonomy

**The withdrawal of 5,000 U.S. troops from Germany is not just a military redeployment; it is a symptom of the unraveling of the post-war transatlantic security architecture.**

## What is being said

A narrative is emerging, highlighted by analysts such as Imran Khalid (via The Atlantic), that the Trump administration's decision to remove 5,000 U.S. service members from Germany represents a "step toward the liquidation of the shared West." This framing suggests that the "shared West" is a cultural and human project of solidarity that is now being dismantled by U.S. unilateralism and transactional diplomacy.

The withdrawal follows public tensions between U.S. President Donald Trump and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, specifically regarding the U.S. approach to Iran. While the German government has publicly downplayed the rift, the physical removal of troops serves as a tangible signal of shifting U.S. priorities.

## What the evidence shows

The Pentagon officially announced the withdrawal of approximately 5,000 troops from Germany on Friday, May 1, 2026, with the process expected to complete within 6 to 12 months (AP News, ABC News). This reduction affects the largest U.S. military presence in Europe.

The move is widely seen as a punitive or transactional response to political disagreements. Chancellor Friedrich Merz has attempted to frame the withdrawal as "anticipated," while simultaneously urging for a stronger, more independent European defense capability (CNBC, NPR).

This pattern confirms a broader trend: the U.S. is increasingly using its security guarantees as leverage in bilateral disputes, transforming the "shared West" from a strategic alliance into a series of transactional contracts.

## European context

For Europe, this is the definitive end of the "security umbrella" era. The "liquidation of the shared West" is a warning that Europe cannot afford to be a junior partner in a project where the senior partner is willing to withdraw based on a personal feud.

This validates the necessity of the "Sovereignty Stack." If the U.S. can remove 5,000 troops over a disagreement about Iran, the security of the European continent cannot remain a variable of U.S. domestic politics. The transition from "NATO-dependence" to "European Strategic Autonomy" is no longer a theoretical policy goal—it is an existential requirement.

The response from Berlin—urging stronger European defense—shows that the realization is hitting home. However, the shift must move beyond rhetoric to the actual integration of command structures and industrial production.
