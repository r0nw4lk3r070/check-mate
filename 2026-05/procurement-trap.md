# The Procurement Trap: Why Europe Must Move Beyond Collaborative Buying

For decades, European defense has been a story of "collaboration" that rarely scales. We create frameworks, we sign MoUs, and we launch "initiatives" like PESCO. But while the terminology has evolved, the core problem remains: Europe buys defense like a collection of boutiques, while its adversaries build like industrial empires.

The current push toward joint procurement—highlighted by the Strategic Compass and the latest PESCO projects—is a necessary first step, but it is not the destination. The goal cannot simply be "buying together to save money." The goal must be the creation of a unified European Defense Industrial Base (EDIB).

## The Fragmentation Tax

Europe currently pays a "fragmentation tax" on every missile, drone, and armored vehicle it produces. When 27 different nations maintain 27 different procurement cycles, 27 different sets of technical requirements, and 27 different national champions, the result is inefficiency. 

This fragmentation doesn't just cost money; it costs time. In the face of the Ukraine conflict, the inability to rapidly scale production of 155mm shells was not a failure of will, but a failure of industrial architecture. We have the engineers and the factories, but we lack the unified command of production.

## From PESCO to a Unified Command

PESCO (Permanent Structured Cooperation) has been vital in building the habit of cooperation. But the shift now must be from *cooperation* (where nations agree to work together) to *integration* (where the system is designed for a single European objective).

Joint procurement is the tool, but Strategic Autonomy is the objective. To achieve this, Europe needs:

1. **Standardization by Default**: No more "national variants" that break interoperability. A European requirement must be the only requirement.
2. **Industrial Consolidation**: Moving beyond the "national champion" model. We need European champions capable of competing with Lockheed Martin or Northrop Grumman on a global scale.
3. **Aggregated Demand**: Using the EU's collective purchasing power to force industry to prioritize European security needs over export markets.

## The Strategic Imperative

The "Steel Porcupine" strategy—making Europe too costly to attack—cannot be built on a patchwork of bilateral deals. It requires an industrial logic that matches the geopolitical reality.

If Europe continues to treat defense procurement as a series of diplomatic compromises, it will remain a security consumer, dependent on the whims of transatlantic partners. To be a geopolitical actor, Europe must first become an industrial power in its own right.

The era of "collaborative buying" must end. The era of integrated European production must begin.
