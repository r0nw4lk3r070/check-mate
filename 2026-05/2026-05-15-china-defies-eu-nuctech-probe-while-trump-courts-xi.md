# China Tells Its Companies to Defy EU Law — While Trump Courts Xi in Beijing

**Date**: 2026-05-15  
**Author**: Watchpost  
**Source**: Watchpost monitoring + open-source intelligence

---

## The Signal

On May 15, 2026, China's Ministry of Justice issued a directive ordering Chinese entities not to comply with the European Union's anti-subsidy investigation into Nuctech, a state-linked Chinese security scanner manufacturer. The ministry called the EU's information demands "arbitrary" and "unjustified extraterritorial jurisdiction."

The move invokes China's newly enacted **Provisions on Countering Foreign Unlawful Extraterritorial Jurisdiction** (State Council Order No. 835), which took effect on April 13, 2026 — just five weeks ago.

This is not a diplomatic protest. It is a legal order telling Chinese companies to defy EU law on EU soil.

## Why It Matters

Nuctech's security scanners are embedded across Europe's critical infrastructure:

- **Brussels Eurostar terminal** — the rail gateway between the EU and the UK
- **Strasbourg Airport** — in the city that houses the European Parliament
- **Ports of Rotterdam and Antwerp** — Europe's two largest seaports
- **Airports in Stockholm, Pisa, and Athens**
- **Border crossings and customs facilities** across at least 26 EU member states

Nuctech is partly owned by **Tsinghua University**, a Chinese state institution. It was **blacklisted by the US Bureau of Industry and Security in 2020**. An Associated Press investigation found that Nuctech's ownership structure traces back to Chinese military and government entities.

The EU opened its in-depth investigation into Nuctech under the **Foreign Subsidies Regulation (FSR)** in December 2025. In April 2024, the European Commission conducted **dawn raids on Nuctech's Dutch and Polish offices** — the first-ever use of FSR raid powers.

Now China is telling its companies: don't cooperate. Don't hand over documents. Don't answer questions. On European territory. About European infrastructure.

## The Context: Two Power Plays, One Absent Player

The timing is not accidental.

On the same day China issued this directive, **Donald Trump was concluding a three-day summit with Xi Jinping in Beijing** — the first US state visit to China in nine years. The summit produced:

- A vague commitment from Xi to help keep the **Strait of Hormuz** open (where 45–50% of China's crude oil imports flow)
- Trump's claim that China would buy **200 Boeing jets** (unconfirmed by Beijing)
- Xi's promise that "China's door will only open wider" to US business
- **No European at the table**, no European interests represented

Meanwhile, China has enacted two new regulations in April 2026 that amount to a legal toolkit for defying foreign regulators:

1. **Provisions on Industrial and Supply Chain Security** (State Council Order No. 834) — authorises retaliation against countries that "discriminate" against China in supply chains
2. **Provisions on Countering Foreign Unlawful Extraterritorial Jurisdiction** (State Council Order No. 835) — the law invoked against the EU Nuctech probe, creating a "Malicious Entity List" and enforcement prohibition orders

These are not theoretical. They are being deployed — against the EU — within weeks of enactment.

## The European Angle

This is a direct challenge to European sovereignty. A foreign power is ordering its companies to defy EU law, using a law enacted five weeks ago, about a company that operates security equipment in the European Parliament's own airport.

The EU's Foreign Subsidies Regulation is one of the most important new tools in the European trade defence arsenal. If China can simply order non-cooperation, the FSR becomes unenforceable against Chinese firms — which are precisely the firms most likely to benefit from foreign subsidies.

The European Commission has not yet responded publicly to the Chinese directive. A hearing in Nuctech's challenge to the 2024 dawn raids is scheduled before the EU General Court.

## What This Means

- **Europe's regulatory sovereignty is being tested.** If the EU cannot investigate foreign subsidies in its own market, the single market is not sovereign.
- **China is building a legal fortress.** The April 2026 regulations create a comprehensive framework for blocking foreign investigations, retaliating against companies that comply, and designating "malicious entities."
- **Trump's Beijing summit underscores the pattern.** The US and China are negotiating directly — on rare earths, AI chips, Taiwan, Hormuz. Europe is not in the room. And while Trump courts Xi, China defies EU law.
- **Nuctech is a test case.** How the EU responds to this directive will determine whether the FSR has teeth — or whether it becomes a dead letter for the most important category of foreign subsidised firms.
