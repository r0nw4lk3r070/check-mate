# RESourceEU: The New Industrial Command for Raw Materials

**Europe is moving from a regulatory approach to a direct interventionist strategy to secure the minerals that power its green and digital future.**

## What is being said
For years, the EU’s approach to critical raw materials (CRMs) was primarily regulatory—setting standards and hoping markets would diversify. However, with China's dominance in the value chain and the increasing use of export controls, the European Commission has shifted gears. The adoption of the **RESourceEU Action Plan** (December 2025) and the subsequent activation of its tools in 2026 signal a transition toward a "security-driven" industrial policy.

## What the evidence shows
The core of this shift is the creation of the **European Critical Raw Materials Centre** in 2026. Inspired by Japan's JOGMEC, this centre is not just a monitoring body but an active agent of industrial policy. It is designed to:
- Develop systemic intelligence on CRM value chains.
- Steer investment and de-risking finance into priority projects.
- Support strategic stockpiling and joint purchasing.

To back this up, the EU is mobilizing **€3 billion in funds** within 12 months for priority projects, such as lithium extraction in Germany. Furthermore, the EU is tightening its "circularity" requirements by proposing restrictions on the export of permanent magnet scraps by Q2 2026 to ensure European recyclers have the necessary feedstock.

Crucially, the EU is now integrating these efforts into its broader security architecture. From May 2026, the **Internal Market Emergency and Resilience Act (IMERA)** allows the Commission to enter "vigilance" or "emergency" modes to coordinate stockpiling and mandate priority requests during supply disruptions.

## European context
RESourceEU is the "Material Basis" of the Sovereignty Stack in action. It represents the end of the era where Europe believed it could simply "buy its way" to a green transition via the Single Market. 

By creating a centralized command for raw materials, the EU is admitting that the "Brussels Effect" (regulatory power) is insufficient when facing a geopolitical competitor that controls the physical atoms of the transition. The shift toward joint purchasing and strategic stockpiling is a move toward a "Federalism of Necessity"—where Member States surrender individual procurement autonomy to achieve collective security. This is the industrial equivalent of the European Defence Union: a recognition that in the 21st century, a mineral shortage is as much a security threat as a military one.
