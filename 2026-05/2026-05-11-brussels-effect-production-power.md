# Beyond the Brussels Effect: The Shift to Production Power

**Europe is transitioning from a "regulatory superpower" to a "production power" to secure its strategic autonomy in an era of geopolitical volatility.**

## What is being said

For years, the "Brussels Effect" — the EU's ability to set global standards through its internal market regulation (e.g., GDPR, AI Act) — was hailed as Europe's primary source of global power. However, a growing consensus among European policymakers and economists, highlighted in the 2024 Draghi Report and the subsequent "Competitiveness Compass" initiatives of 2026, suggests that regulation alone is insufficient. 

The narrative is shifting: the EU can no longer rely solely on "normative power" (the power to set rules) while remaining dependent on the US and China for the actual production of the technologies those rules govern. There is an urgent call to bridge the gap between regulatory ambition and industrial capacity.

## What the evidence shows

The 2024 Draghi Report explicitly warned that Europe's productivity gap with the US is widening, which undermines its strategic autonomy. To restore competitiveness, Draghi estimated an additional investment of €800 billion annually is required. 

By March 2026, the European Council agreed on a concrete agenda to strengthen competitiveness and resilience. This involves a transition from a purely regulatory approach to a "production-first" strategy. Key pillars include:
- **The Net-Zero Industry Act (NZIA)** and **Chips Act**: Moving from setting environmental standards to ensuring the actual manufacturing of solar panels, batteries, and semiconductors happens within the EU.
- **Critical Raw Materials Act (CRM Act)**: Addressing the "vicious circle" of dependency by diversifying supply chains and scaling internal recycling and mining, recognizing that without the material basis, regulatory goals for the energy transition are unattainable.
- **The "Competitiveness Compass" (April 2026)**: A reconfiguration of the European project that treats strategic autonomy not as a set of industrial policies, but as a constitutional necessity for survival in a world of "geopolitical shocks."

The evidence suggests that while the AI Act (2024) sets the global gold standard for AI ethics, Europe's lack of homegrown foundational models creates a "sovereignty gap" that regulation cannot fill.

## European context

This shift represents the "maturation" of European strategic autonomy. For decades, the EU operated on the belief that the Single Market's size was its greatest asset. In 2026, the reality is that market size is useless if the supply chains are controlled by external actors who can weaponize dependencies.

From a pro-European perspective, this is the move toward a "United States of Europe" in the economic sense: a bloc that not only regulates its citizens but produces the tools of its own future. Moving from the "Brussels Effect" (regulating others) to "Production Power" (building for ourselves) is the only way to ensure that European values—democracy, sustainability, and the rule of law—are backed by material reality.
