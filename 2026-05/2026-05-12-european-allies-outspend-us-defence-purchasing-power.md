# US allies now outspend America on defence in purchasing power

**For the first time, America's treaty allies spent more on defence than the US itself when adjusted for purchasing power — and European rearmament is the driving force.**

## What is being said

A new analysis by The Economist, using SIPRI's 2025 global military expenditure data, calculates that US treaty allies (31 non-US NATO members plus Japan, South Korea, Thailand, Australia, and the Philippines) spent 111% of the US defence budget in purchasing-power-adjusted terms in 2025. This is the first time allies have crossed the 100% threshold.

European NATO members and Canada alone reached 81% of US spending — still short of parity, but catching up rapidly. The data was published alongside SIPRI's annual report on 27 April 2026, which recorded global military spending at $2,887 billion, the 11th consecutive year of growth.

## What the evidence shows

Key data points from SIPRI and The Economist's analysis:

- **Global military spending** reached $2,887 billion in 2025, up 2.9% from 2024. The increase would have been much larger (9.2%) if not for a 7.5% drop in US spending, driven mainly by the absence of new Ukraine military aid appropriations.

- **European NATO members** spent a combined $559 billion in 2025. 22 of 29 European NATO members now spend at least 2% of GDP on defence. European spending grew at the fastest rate since 1953.

- **Germany** became the largest European NATO spender at $114 billion, a 24% year-on-year increase. Germany's military burden hit 2.3% of GDP — the first time above 2% since 1990.

- **Spain** increased military spending by 50% to $40.2 billion, also crossing the 2% threshold for the first time since 1994.

- **Russia** spent $190 billion (7.5% of GDP, the highest military burden recorded). **Ukraine** spent $84.1 billion (40% of GDP).

- **US spending** fell to $954 billion in 2025, but SIPRI notes that Congress has already approved over $1 trillion for 2026, and Trump's latest budget proposal could push it to $1.5 trillion by 2027.

SIPRI researcher Jade Guiberteau Ricard noted that European NATO spending rose faster than at any time since 1953, "reflecting the ongoing pursuit of European self-reliance alongside increasing pressure from the United States to strengthen burden sharing within the alliance."

## European context

This data tells a story that European policymakers have been trying to make visible for years: Europe is not free-riding. The narrative that American allies don't pay their share has been a persistent political weapon, but the purchasing-power-adjusted numbers now show allies collectively outspending the US.

The catch is in the word "collectively." No single European state matches US spending, and European defence remains fragmented across 27+ national budgets, procurement systems, and strategic cultures. The spending is real — the cohesion is not.

This is precisely why the European Commission's White Paper on Defence and the push for a genuine European Defence Union matter. The money is arriving. The question is whether it becomes a coherent European capability or stays a collection of national chequebooks.

Germany crossing the 2% threshold is symbolically significant. For decades, Germany was the ally most frequently accused of under-investing. At 2.3% of GDP and $114 billion, that chapter is closing — but Germany's own target of 3.5% by 2029 signals that the trajectory is still accelerating.

The SIPRI data also underscores a darker reality: Russia's military burden is 7.5% of GDP. Even as European spending rises, the Russian state continues to pour resources into its war machine at levels that dwarf European commitments as a share of national output. The spending race is not won yet.
