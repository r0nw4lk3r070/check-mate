# Three Stories, One Absence: Europe Wasn't in the Room

**Date**: 2026-05-14  
**Author**: Watchpost  
**Classification**: Amplify  

---

While Europeans slept, three events converged on May 13-14 that redefine the continent's security — and not one of them featured a European voice at the table.

## 1. Hungary Summoned Russia's Ambassador — For the First Time

When Russia launched more than 800 drones and missiles at Ukraine on May 13, the barrage reached Zakarpattia Oblast — Ukraine's westernmost region, which shares a direct border with Hungary and is home to a significant ethnic Hungarian minority. Infrastructure in Svaliava and Uzhhorod was struck. Slovakia closed its border. Moldovan airspace was violated.

Hungary's new Prime Minister Péter Magyar, leading the Tisza government in its first cabinet meeting, did something Viktor Orbán never did: he condemned Russia by name and summoned the Russian ambassador.

> "The Hungarian government strongly condemns the Russian attack on Zakarpattia Oblast."

Foreign Minister Anita Orbán was directed to meet the Russian envoy on the morning of May 14 to "condemn the attack and ask when Russia plans to end the war."

This is a seismic shift. Under Orbán, Hungary was Russia's most reliable advocate inside the EU and NATO. That era is over — not because of diplomatic persuasion, but because Russian drones reached the Hungarian border.

**Sources**: Defcon Level (EUCOM alert), Guardian Ukraine war briefing, Kyiv Independent

## 2. Russia Legalised Future Invasions

On the same day, Russia's State Duma passed a bill — in both second and third readings — granting the president the right to use armed forces abroad to "protect Russian citizens." The legislation amends Russia's citizenship and defence laws.

The bill was drafted by the Defence Ministry and introduced on March 19. It passed its first reading on April 14. The speed is telling.

Independent Russian outlet Meduza noted that the law's purpose is "not entirely clear" — Putin already had authority under Article 8 of Federal Law No. 390 to "take measures" protecting citizens when foreign bodies make decisions contradicting Russia's interests. The head of the Duma's defence committee tied the bill to the case of a Russian archaeologist detained in Poland — a flimsy pretext for a law that echoes the 2014 Crimea annexation's "protecting Russian speakers" justification.

More practically, Kommersant reported that lawyers suggest the law could provide legal cover for Russian Navy vessels escorting shadow fleet tankers under sanctions. The Bell noted that Russian lawmakers modelled the bill on the U.S. "Hague Invasion Act" (ASPA, 2002) — legislation designed to shield American officials from the ICC.

The ICC has issued arrest warrants for Vladimir Putin, Maria Lvova-Belova, and senior Russian military figures. This law is a legal framework for future aggression — whether against neighbouring states or in service of sanctions-busting fleets.

**Sources**: Meduza (TASS report), Kommersant, The Bell, State Duma records

## 3. Trump Met Xi in Beijing — Europe Was Not Invited

Across the globe, Donald Trump arrived in Beijing for a 36-hour summit with Xi Jinping — the first visit by a U.S. president to China since Trump's own 2017 trip. The agenda: rare earths, AI chips, Taiwan, and Iran. More than a dozen U.S. CEOs accompanied him, including Nvidia's Jensen Huang, added at the last minute after Trump called him directly.

Politico called it "the shrinking summit." Analysts at the Council on Foreign Relations concluded the two leaders are "pursuing very different objectives" — Trump needs visible deliverables before November's midterms; Xi operates on a longer timeline and arrives having already used rare earths to force U.S. concessions during the 2025 trade war. Chatham House described the U.S. list of concrete deliverables as "short" and noted a "shared preference for managing their competition rather than resolving it."

On Taiwan, Trump told reporters that Xi "would like us not to" sell weapons to Taipei and that he'd "have that discussion" — phrasing that alarmed officials in Taipei and should alarm every European capital that depends on U.S. security commitments.

On rare earths, China processes 85%+ of global output. For 12 critical elements — including terbium and dysprosium, essential for F-35 jets, EV motors, missile guidance, and AI hardware — China's share exceeds 90%. Beijing already demonstrated its leverage in April 2025, when rare earth magnet exports to the U.S. fell 93% year-on-year. The suspension of the second wave of controls expires in November 2026.

On AI chips, Commerce Secretary Lutnick told lawmakers in April 2026 that no H200 chips had been exported to China despite December 2025 licensing — because the Chinese government blocked its own companies from purchasing them. Two gatekeepers, not one.

No European official was at the table. No EU representative was in the room. The two powers deciding the future of critical mineral supply chains, semiconductor access, and Pacific security did so without the continent that depends on both.

**Sources**: Politico, Council on Foreign Relations, Chatham House, TechTimes, CNBC, IEA, CSIS, Wall Street Journal, Foreign Policy

## What This Means for Europe

These three stories share a single, uncomfortable thread: Europe's security order is being rewritten, and Europe isn't writing it.

- Russia escalates drone warfare to record levels and simultaneously legalises future invasions — on the same day.
- Hungary, once Russia's closest EU ally, breaks with Moscow because drones hit its border — not because of diplomatic persuasion.
- The U.S. and China negotiate the terms of the 21st century's critical supply chains without European input.

The lesson is plain. When Russia's drones reach your border, the calculus changes. When the U.S. president discusses your security architecture with his Chinese counterpart over dinner in Beijing, you are not at the table. When the legal framework for the next invasion is passed in a single day, the threat is not hypothetical.

Europe's response must be structural, not rhetorical. More defence spending — yes, but also more defence sovereignty. More industrial capacity — yes, but also more strategic independence in critical minerals, semiconductors, and energy. More political unity — yes, but also more willingness to act when the moment demands it, not after.

The drones reached Zakarpattia. The invasion law passed the Duma. The summit happened in Beijing.

What are we waiting for?
